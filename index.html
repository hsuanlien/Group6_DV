Observations

Elevation notes

  • High-elevation airports are geographically concentrated rather than globally distributed.
  • Route volume shows no clear monotonic relationship with airport elevation.
  • Aircraft usage is less diverse at high-elevation airports.
  • High-elevation airports show a more concentrated aircraft mix, with a few types taking large shares.
  • Aircraft type distribution changes across elevation groups, while route volume varies widely at many elevations.

Hubs notes

  • Atlanta (ATL) is the busiest departing hub in the dataset with 915 departing routes.
  • Most major hubs are concentrated in North America and Europe, including ATL, ORD, LHR, CDG, and FRA.
  • Asia also hosts key global hubs, such as PEK, that support long-haul and intercontinental connectivity.

Distance notes

  • Most flight routes are short-haul. The majority of routes are under 1,500 km, showing that regional flights dominate global air traffic.
  • Long-distance flights are much less common. The distance distribution is right-skewed, with only a small number of very long-haul routes.
  • Short-haul routes remain mostly regional, whereas long-haul routes span continents and oceans.

Curvature notes

  • Most long-distance routes follow mid-latitude or polar-like paths.
  • Equatorial-like routes are rare among long-haul flights.
